Realistic Research: University, Web-based, and Local Resources

This lesson is meant for students who must begin research in their first semester of the TCID, IA, or the Ph.D. in Technical Communication program who want to develop, improve, or refresh their research skills. Additionally, this lesson is designed to give students strategies which will help them research more efficiently.

This lesson will help you learn to acquire research resources through a review of the following areas:

  • Getting Started
    • Learn how to access IIT library services
    • Learn basic functionality on the IIT library website
  • Research Timelines

Getting Started

1. Accessing Library Services

If you are a newly admitted student and have not yet obtained an IIT Hawk Card, details for doing so can be found at the Hawk Card Homepage. Your Hawk Card number is required for accessing certain library services.

Note: Even if you don’t have a Hawk Card, you can still access online journals and perform all search functions through the library website. You will only need your Hawk Card to check out materials in person at the Galvin Library or request materials through interlibrary loan.

2. Using myIIT to Access the Library Website

The recommended way to access the library website is by first logging into myIIT. Here’s how:

  1. Log in to your myIIT account.
  2. Once logged in, look for the link to Library Resources.
  3. Click on the Library Resources link to be redirected to the library search website.

Tip: Accessing the library website through myIIT provides a proxy server connection, enabling you to access most resources without needing to re-enter your login information.

3. Navigating the Library Website and Using Scholarly Databases

After reaching the library website, you’ll find several search options available on the left-hand navigation bar. The best place to start your research is with a scholarly database that allows you to search multiple journals at once, such as:

  • Academic Search Premiere (EBSCO) – a robust database for finding peer-reviewed articles.

Research Timelines

1. Formulating a Timeline

It is important to first consider your due date when thinking about requesting resources. You must have enough time to read the material, integrate it into your project or paper, and still have time to finish your assignment. In general, consider this formula when determining whether it is realistic to use a resource for your paper:

Time to obtain resource + Time to read resource + Time to integrate resource into your project < Remaining time before deadline

Let’s unpack some of those terms:

  • Time to obtain resource: How long will it take for the resource to arrive via interlibrary loan? Or, how long will it take for you to physically obtain the resource?
  • Time to read resource: How long will it take you to read the book or article? Are you at a point in your project where you must begin to write if you want to finish on time?
  • Time to integrate resource into your project: Even if you are able to read the resource, do you have enough time to identify pertinent information and incorporate that information into your project? If your paper is already written, do you have enough time to restructure it to allow for discussion of this resource?
  • Remaining time before deadline: How much time is left before you must turn in your project? If the resource is powerful enough to cause you to rethink your approach to your topic, requesting an extension might be necessary.

Being Realistic

Don’t assume that you can read an entire scholarly book in one day. It’s better to set realistic goals and avoid wasting precious time on resources you can’t fully utilize.

Case Study: Example of a Research Timeline

Bridgette, a graduate student in Tech Comm, was assigned a literature review and research paper. She decided to research the development of technical communication as a field between the early 20th century and World War II.

To start, Bridgette logged into myIIT and searched for articles using databases like EBSCO and JSTOR. She found pertinent articles but couldn't access them directly. To determine if it was worthwhile to request these, she constructed a research timeline.

Bridgette set reasonable goals in her Google calendar to track when each part of her project should be completed. Starting with enough time, she planned to request, read, and integrate each resource well before her deadline.

2. Determining the Best Way to Obtain Resources

Even if you can obtain a resource, it’s essential to decide whether it’s worth the time. Preview resources through reviews or Google Books to ensure they’re valuable. If a resource fits within your timeline, consider these options:

  • The stated turnaround time: Consider whether you’d still have time to use the resource if it took twice the estimated time to arrive.
  • The proximity of the lending library: If you’re using WorldCat, your lending library could be far away, so delivery may take time. Always check the library’s location before requesting materials.
  • The possibility of delay: If the resource might not arrive in time, it’s better to select another topic or source that’s more accessible.

Requesting Resources

1. Requesting Articles

Clicking on the Get Article link will direct you to a page like this, where you will either be able to retrieve the article through another website or where you will request the article through your myILL account.

Your myILL account requires a separate setup from your myIIT account, so you will need to register before you are able to request an article. Think of myILL as asking a friend to make a copy of an article for you. For more help requesting articles, see the Library FAQ and How-to Pages.

2. Requesting a Book from Another Library

When searching for a book, it is best to begin by searching the IIT library system. If you are unsuccessful in finding the title you are looking for, next try to locate the book in I-Share. If I-Share yields no results, the next step is to search for your book in WorldCat.

Being Realistic

When collecting resources, don’t assume that you will remember any of the titles you look up or that you will be able to find them again using past searches. Keep a list of resources on Google Documents or on your flash drive.

If you are confused when using any of the library systems, refer back to the Library FAQ and How-to Pages, which have tutorials explaining the step-by-step instructions for requesting resources.

Case Study: Searching and Requesting Resources

Bridgette receives the articles that she has requested and reads through them. One of the resources that Jane finds frequently quoted in academic papers is the 1923 text English for Engineers written by S. A. Harbarger. She decides that this is a valuable resource for her project and records the title, author, and year in her Google Docs resource list.

After a quick search in I-Share, she finds it available at the University of Illinois library in Chicago. She makes a note of this in her resources list. Although Bridgette is working on another project, she knows she can return to her research notes and resume work when she’s ready.

Local Libraries

There are two types of local libraries that may contain your resources: other university libraries belonging to I-Share and public libraries.

1. I-Share Libraries

Many I-Share libraries allow you to pull books directly from the shelf and check them out if you present your IIT ID card and have no holds on your library account. Daley Library at the University of Illinois at Chicago (UIC) has an open stacks policy. UIC is also a Research I institution, so their library is quite extensive.

However, not all area libraries participate in the I-Share system. For example, Loyola University and the University of Chicago do not participate in I-Share. You may be able to check out books, but under certain conditions, and you may not have access to browse their collections.

If you plan to visit an I-Share library to obtain a resource, consider the following:

  • Where is the library located? Will you need to travel outside of Chicago? If so, consider using interlibrary loan.
  • Can you enter the library? Although you’re a student at an I-Share institution, different libraries have varying rules for who can browse book stacks.
  • What are the library hours? Not every institution uses the semester system. For instance, DePaul University uses the quarter system, so check their website to verify library hours.

Being Realistic

Don't waste your research time by traveling to a library only to find it closed or that browsing is restricted. When in doubt, call first.

2. Public Libraries

The Chicago Public Library (CPL) system is extensive and includes many books in high demand or hard to find at I-Share libraries, such as textbooks. If you live outside of Chicago, you cannot check out books, but you can visit and read or copy them. Most local libraries have similar policies. To obtain a CPL card, visit a CPL location with proof of residency.

Case Study: Obtaining a Resource from an I-Share Library

After finishing another project, Bridgette returns to her research paper on Tech Comm prior to World War II. She notices she had made a note to obtain English for Engineers by S. A. Harbarger, but now only has ten days left to finish her paper. Bridgette reassesses her research timeline and decides she has enough time to read, analyze, and incorporate this resource, but she needs to obtain it immediately.

Seeing her note that the book is available at the UIC library, Bridgette gathers the title, author, local library, and call number and prepares to visit. Before she leaves, however, she checks the UIC library's hours and policies to avoid a wasted trip.

When to Use Local Library Resources Instead of Interlibrary Loan

There are two situations where you should consider obtaining a resource from a local library rather than through interlibrary loan:

  • You no longer have enough time to wait for an interlibrary loan book to arrive.
  • The book is unavailable through interlibrary loan.

Remember that CPL volumes are not listed in I-Share holdings. Even though public libraries are located in Illinois, they are not part of the I-Share system. You must search on the CPL website to view available titles.

Internet Resources

1. Some Resources to Consider

If you can obtain the same resource online, why request it? Some resources available online include:

  • Public domain writings: Accessible without any cost or restriction.
  • Resources licensed under Creative Commons: Often freely available for educational use.
  • Media files: Such as video, audio, and images that may complement your research.

In general, if you can avoid making a trip to the library, you can save yourself time when researching.

2. Using Web Resources for Research

These web resources can help you find additional resources:

  • Google Scholar: Allows you to search for scholarly articles and view which articles are cited by other articles.
  • Google Books: Allows you to preview a book, or in some cases, view the entire book, including the references section.
  • Web of Science: Similar to Google Scholar, but focused on scientific papers.

Being Realistic

If you're stuck when researching a topic in Technical Communication, try searching for a special issue, yearbook, or edited volume on your topic. These collections provide a snapshot of collective scholarship on a topic, helping you see how your topic relates to other research.

Case Study: Using Web Resources

Near the end of her project, Bridgette questions some arguments in a central article in her paper, "The Rise of Technical Writing Instruction in America" by R.J. Connors (1982). She wants to see if other researchers included examples of technical writing instruction before World War II. Assuming many articles would have referenced Connors' work, she searches Google Scholar using his article title and finds it has been cited 62 times. From there, she reviews articles that cite Connors to find examples relevant to her research.
